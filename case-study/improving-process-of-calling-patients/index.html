Improving the process of making reminder calls to patients

Conducted in August – September 2020

How might we improve the number of hypertensive patients who return for their follow-up visit? This is the larger challenge that the team was designing for.

Study objective

Understand the process and experience of making reminder calls to patients.

Methods
  • Generative research
  • Diary study
  • Remote interviews
Outcome
  • Designed interventions to improve and enhance the experience of calling patients.
  • Created a clear, shared understanding of the calling process in the team.

Hypertension is a long-term condition that needs patients to return for regular check-up. Getting patients to return to care is a key area of focus for national hypertension control programs in India and Bangladesh. One way that this is achieved is through making reminder calls to patients.

Our team at Obvious, Resolve to Save Lives and National Heart Foundation of Bangladesh was working for the goal of optimizing the process of calling patients — that is, how can calling patients be made more effective and successful?

To kick things off, we started with foundational research to understand the current process of calling patients and the challenges faced. Here, I’ll take you through the research process and the outcome:

Creating the plan

Defining objectives

Step one, as in all studies, is to define the objectives. This works well when they are set as a team.

Our objectives were to:

  • Understand the staff’s process of calling patients.
  • Learn about their experience and the pain points.
  • Learn about the patients’ responses.

For each of these objectives, we defined the research sub-questions.

Reruiting participants

The participants for the study were staff members at the National Heart Foundation of Bangladesh who call patients as part of their job. The study was conducted with 5 participants.

Note: National Heart Foundation of Bangladesh was also a partner and stakeholder in this project.

Choosing methods

We chose the methods of a diary study along with in-depth interviews. These would help us get deeper and more accurate insights for our objectives. Note that in-person studies were not possible due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

While designing the study, it was important to keep in mind the participants’ comfort level with digital tools and with English. I chose Google Docs as our tool for creating the diary — participants were familiar with it and onboarding was not required. The instructions were worded in simple and friendly English.

Writing the plan

The project plan we wrote included the timeline, objectives, interview guide and supporting resources. Apart from being the go-to reference for the study, it also acted as a place where team members collaborate and align on the details of the study.

Conducting the study

The study consisted of a week-long diary study and subsequent in-depth remote interviews.

1. Diary study

The diary study started with a video call to brief the participants. The participants learned about the purpose of the study, and tested that they could work with their online diaries. They were asked to log details of the phone calls they made to patients, every day, for a week.

In the initial briefing call, it was important to build rapport and ensure that all participants felt motivated for the week-long task at hand.

As the diary study started, I reviewed their inputs and corresponded asychronously over email. We had a mid-week video call for a check-in. The study proceeded smoothly.

2. In-depth remote interviews

At the end of the week, I conducted interviews. This was the time to learn deeply and in detail about the participants' process.

The interviews had three main sections:

  • Understand their end-to-end process of calling.
  • Clarify and probe deeper into the responses in the diaries.
  • Generate ideas for improvement with the participants.

Again, it was important to create a comfortable and engaging environment for the participants — so that what we learned was accurate and meaningful for the project.

There are many limitations of conducting remote interviews. The format, however, presents its own afforances that improve the quality of the interviews. For instance, taking notes on an experience map template while interviewing the participants, helped participants feel more engaged and heard.

Synthesis

To synthesise, we went over the filled-in and further annotated diaries, and notes and artifacts from the interviews. We coded them with key themes. We then did a second pass and moved the important notes onto digital post-its on Figma. Following the process of affinity diagramming, they were clustured into groups with similar themes and ideas. From here, we derived the key insights of the study.

What we learned

We learned of the details of how staff members make calls to patients. These details included when the calls are made, from which locations and environments, the series of steps followed and the tools used in the end-to-end process.

We got insight into how the calls are worded, the average duration of each call, and how patients in rural Sylhet, Bangladesh respond on the phone calls.

Learning about the challenges faced led to us identifying design opportunities. In addition, participants' inputs helped us imagine and concretely define what an "easy" call would be like — what would good user experience of calling patients be?

Outcome

This study helped create a clear, shared understanding in the team of the process of making reminder calls to patients. It gave the team insight, motivation and direction for improving the process.

We added features to the Simple app and web dashboard to enhance the experience of calling patients. These features would 1) reduce the manual work involved in the process and 2) support the caller in making more informed calls.

Based on the findings and using the existing knowledge in the team, we created a Standard Operating Procedure — a set of best practices for making effective and successful reminder calls to patients. This would be a resource for all healthcare workers part of the project who make reminder calls to patients.

Participating in the study was energising for the staff who we interviewed. It helped them identify the gaps in their own processes and put improvements in place on the field.

Making reminder calls to patients is a key intervention used by national health programs in India and Bangladesh to improve large-scale hypertension control numbers. This foundational study helped initiate long-term improvements for this work for our team.
