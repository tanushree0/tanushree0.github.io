Building a user research practice for a product team

The challenge

Simple is an app that is designed to help clinicians manage hypertensive patients. It is deployed in 1033 public health facilities in India and Bangladesh, and is used to manage over 350,000 patients. It is supported by Resolve to Save Lives, and built by a team with people from Resolve to Save Lives, Obvious and Nilenso, and public health experts from various organisations.

The challenge for the project was to design a product that is easy to learn and use, even for first-time smartphone users. It needed to fit into the existing, extremely busy workflows in public health facilities.

As the users’ contexts are very different from ours, it was important to build a strong user research practice to set up the project for success. I worked on the project as part of the design team at Obvious, as the primary user researcher. Here I describe our team’s key user research processes:

Continuous user studies

We conducted continuous user studies — on the field, in our office, and remotely. The studies were both generative and evaluative. Our methods included contextual inquiry, interviews, concept testing, usability testing, phone surveys, co-creation and diary studies.

Over two years, we interviewed above 250 clinicians and stakeholders. Iterative user research meant that we stayed in tune with the product team’s process and provided continuous strategic insight. The continuous feedback loop with users shaped the product from its beta stages to as it scaled up.

Designing with users

Many users we were designing for were first-time smartphone users. They included nurses, doctors, pharmacists and counselors working in rural and remote parts of the country.

For the app to be adopted, it needed to align with the mental models and contexts of the users. To this end, we included co-creation and participatory design activities in user studies. This helped us understand users’ ways of thinking and their needs.

While working with health workers, we listened deeply and created safe spaces for them to speak. We were sensitive to the local and cultural norms. While gathering feedback, we used play-based activities. These approaches helped users share their perspectives authentically and engage in participatory design processes.

Creating a user-centred culture

Our users' contexts were very different from our team’s. So it was important to bring the knowledge of users’ context to the team in a way that they could understand and act upon it.

For each field study, 1–2 members of the engineering team joined us (before COVID-19, of course!). We coached them on user research ethics, how to observe, and created small research activities for them so they could engage actively on the field.

On a regular basis we conducted team bodystorming sessions. In these, we recreated a busy public health facility and team members role-played as nurses, doctors, and patients. Apart from being enjoyable, these were useful in gaining a deep understanding of users’ contexts.

For any research project, we ensured that the team was well-informed at each stage — planning, execution, and synthesis.

Quantitative along with qualitative

As the design team we identified the key metrics that aligned with the product goals. Working closely with the engineering team, we set up Mixpanel dashboards to track these metrics. This helped with answering key questions on product efficiency and adoption.

We conducted satisfaction surveys over the phone with 6–8 users each month. These gave us a regular pulse check of users’ sentiment and how the product was faring on the field. Collating results of these surveys every six months provided the team with clear direction.

Outcome

Our work helped develop a strong user-centred culture in the team. Engineers visiting the field and bodystorming activities helped inculcate a deeper understanding of the users’ context and their pain points. Through iterative user research, we developed a strong communication loop between the product team and users.

The app won several prestigious design awards, including Best in Show at the Interaction Design awards at Milan, Italy in February 2020.

Customers and stakeholders find the app immensely valuable for their work. Even though many clinicians have limited experience of using technology at their workplace, they use the app with ease while being in their busy work environment.

The adoption of the app consistently grew in the tough environments of public health clinics in India, and expanded to many states in India and to Bangladesh. As of November 2020, the app is deployed in 1033 public health facilities and is used to manage over 300,000 patients.
