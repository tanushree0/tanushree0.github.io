Testing an intervention for reducing salt in home-cooked food

How might we enable behaviour change of consuming home-cooked food with 50% less salt? This is the long-term goal that the team was designing for, through a design sprint.

Study objective

Evaluate the efficacy of messages delivered to rural households for reducing salt in home-cooked food

Methods
  • Concept testing
  • Field study
  • Interviewing
Outcome

Based on the insights gained, the team finalised the strategy for the intervention

This test was part of a 5-day design sprint at Obvious with the clients Resolve to Save Lives and George Institute for Global Health.

Our team designed an intervention to communicate messages to rural households on reducing salt in home-cooked food. Reducing salt intake is beneficial for controlling blood pressure and reducing the risk of cardio-vascular diseases.

These messages were focused on building awareness and providing actionable strategies, and were in the form of posters and short videos. They would be delivered by an ASHA worker (i.e. a community health worker) of the area to people at their homes.

The goal was to test the delivery of the intervention and provide clear direction to the team on the designed strategy.

Planning the test

During the sprint, we deliberated upon who the target for the intervention should be. Should it the women in families who primarily cook the food? Should it be their husbands who are usually the key decision makers? Should it be elder members who hold influence in the family? Or should it be the influencers in the village who the community would listen to?

After further discussion, we defined our target audience to be young couples between the ages of 25â€“40, of medium to higher income group, who were residents of two different villages and from a mix of nuclear and joint families.

The intervention consisted of an ASHA worker delivering the health message to the couples at their homes. We would test the concept through observation of the delivery of the message and using interviews.

The primary objectives for this study were: 1) to evaluate the success of the message delivery, 2) to understand the reasons people would be open to reducing use of salt, and 3) to identify if the strategy of gradual salt reduction was perceived as actionable.

One part of the team worked hard to create the prototypes in time, and the other prepared to test them and traveled to the location. Per our usual practice, we conducted a pilot test within the team before taking it to the field.

Conducting the test

We visited the homes of 6 couples in a district in Telangana. For each visit, I was accompanied by one member from the stakeholder's team who observed and participated as a translator.

The interview started with explaining the purpose, obtaining consent, and building rapport. The initial interview questions were picked to build rapport as well as to lead into a discussion on their cooking practices and preferences.

The ASHA worker, who was a part of our team, shared the message with each couple. The message was delivered, per a pre-decided script, using the designed artifacts of videos and posters.

We observed as the couple received the message and interacted with the ASHA worker. We then interviewed them to evaluate as well as to learn. We evaluated the efficacy of the components of the messsages delivered. We learned deeply about their current attitudes and behaviours related to salt consumption.

The next day, we visited the participants and conducted very short interviews. We learned which messsages had stuck with them after one day, and if they had cooked differently the previous night.

Again, it was important to build rapport and ask questions in the right way, to ensure that we get authentic and accurate responses.

Synthesis

The field study team made detailed notes from the recordings of the interviews.

The message delivery we were evaluating had five different components. For each component, we collated findings from each interview. Patterns emerged in the findings.

For the part of the interview that was generative, we coded the interviews to clarify the themes and conducted affinity mapping to synthesise.

These findings were then presented to the entire team of stakeholders. The team learned about the behaviours and attitudes of their target audience for the project. They understood which components of the message worked well and why, and which components didn't work and why.

Outcome

The test provided a clear direction to the team for moving forward.

Four in five of the messages designed to raise awareness and suggest strategies worked well. They only needed further refinement. One message was not understood correctly and did not align with the participants' current cooking practices. Particular messages had higher recall than others.

The medium of short videos and posters, created in the local language, with locally based characters and references, worked well. The intervention of the ASHA worker delivering the message to couples also worked well. The residents of the villages trusted the ASHA worker of their area and were open to receiving the awareness message from her.

We learned about the participant's current cooking practices, perspectives about use of salt in food, and attitudes and barriers towards making changes in how much salt they consume.

The stakeholders were active participants through the sprint process. Having gained these insights from the tests, they were now confident in moving forward with the designed intervention. They zeroed in on the messages to be developed for their large-scale salt reducation program, and decided next steps for further research.

Team

Our team consisted of Pragati Mehrotra and Prajakta Digambar at Obvious, and stakeholders from Resolve to Save Lives and The George Insititute of Global Health. My role was of a user researcher.
