Designing onboarding of a healthcare app

As the Simple project expanded to more states, we needed to build an effective onboarding flow for the app.

Study Objective

Test the onboarding flow for usability and efficacy

Methods
  • Usability testing
  • Prototyping
Outcome

Developed design for onboarding that was used by over 1000 users.

The deployment of the app was expanding to more districts and states in public health clinics in India. It was key that users be able to quickly onboard to the app.

Can a healthcare worker, on installing the app, start to use it confidently within 15 minutes? This was the goal of the project and what we needed to test for.

My role in this project was that of a user researcher. I also built the prototypes we tested with, with Framer.

Plan the test

The prototype would be tested with ~6 participants in each study. The participants were nurses who work in hospitals in Bangalore and measure BP of patients in their daily work. All of them owned smartphones.

The interviews were designed to create rapport and for flow. This was achieved through the structuring of the interview, use of role-play and props, and facilitation.

We tested two designs in two studies. In the first design, the first-time user goes through a tour of the app. In the second design, the user learns the app through a training video.

Conduct the test

The tests were conducted at the Obvious office in Bangalore. My colleague and I played the roles of the note-taker and interviewer. We live-streamed the interviews for our colleagues using a video conferencing tool.

The initial part of the interview set the context. The participant shared details of their daily work, workflow and use of technology.

We created a make-believe scenario in which the participant was asked by their hospital administrator to start using the app for work. We observed them as they went through the onboarding flow and thought aloud.

Their knowledge of the app was tested through questions and realistic tasks.

Users refrained from clicking on the button called "Take a tour" — the phrase was unfamiliar and was not inviting. A short training video with content that was relateable worked very well.

Analysis

For each feature we were testing for, we listed observations and issues for each participant. From this, we drew patterns on what was working well in the designs and what wasn't.

We prioritised issues based on severity and frequency. From here, we identified potential fixes and design recommendations.

Once the report was created, the team met. We briefed them on the user test — participant behaviours, key findings and design recommendations. The team then identified next steps for design and research.

Outcome

Information consumption through videos worked well for all the participants. In the final design, we reduced the length of the video to be about 5 minutes.

We did not go ahead with the ideas of a tutorial based on 'tour' and coachmarks. These paradigms were not familiar for the user base and would not scale well.

We learned that digital literacy varies in this section of users. Some participants were technically savvy and were active users of their apps. Other participants did not have a basic vocabulary for use of apps — for example, they do not understand how search works or how buttons work.

Later, through various channels such as field studies, phone surveys and stakeholder interactions, we learned that the training video was impactful for introducing new users to the app.

At the same time, it became increasingly clear that the project would need to invest in creating a scaleable solution for onboarding and training. Many users are beginner users of smartphones and will need hands-on training.

Lessons for building for 'Next Billion Users'

1. Align copy with users' mental model.

For example, the CTA text of "Take a tour", although is found commonly in consumer apps, did not work for our target users.

"When it says, 'Take a tour', I don't understand where it will go." — Participant

Another example is where we replaced the button text, "Got it!" to "Okay". Users did not understand what "Got it" meant.

2. Short videos work well for content dissemination.

Consuming videos through apps like Youtube, JioTV and Whatsapp is common among new users of smartphones who we interacted with. We consistently observed that users were comfortable with watching videos on their phones.

3. If it makes sense for your context, invest in training.

For many users among the Next Billion Users, working with apps is unfamiliar and challenging. They do not have an understanding of basic design patterns such as Search, Save, Set password, Settings, etc.

Investing in training resources and hand-on teaching can be essential in some scenarios for your product to grow.
